Predicting Restaurant Health Violations

This study attempts to predict restaurant inspection violations using data available on the review site Yelp. Predicting inspections could help inform the public and help local health departments become more effective by better targeting scarce inspection resources. Both actions could reduce hospital trips, improve health outcomes, and reduce health expenditures. We find that our best predictive model improves accuracy by 10% over baseline accuracy, a relatively small improvement.

Education Research

Professor Zachary Pardos, UC Berkeley

Project Description

State of the art deep learning technologies have been applied to everything from marketing to video recommendations to a natural language processing virtual assistant, but relatively very little advancements have been made in the field of education. We seek to leverage this technology to answer 4 major questions about online learning:

  1. What are the modal archetypes of open online learners?
  2. Do these archetypes generalize across platforms?
  3. Do these archetypes have significant interactions with how effectively students learn from different types of online learning objects?
  4. Can instruction be adapted based on these findings towards improving student performance outcomes?

Consult Your Community

About

Consult Your Community is a student-run organization that mobilizes and trains undergraduates to provide pro bono consulting services to low-income and minority-owned small businesses in college communities across America.

National
Berkeley Chapter
President

When I entered my role as president, CYC was undergoing a very tough transitioning period. The original founders of the organization had all graduated and our identity in the UC Berkeley consulting space was still unclear. My first semester, I wanted to create an internship program that would deliver a reliable pipeline for recruiting and training talent. My second semester, I really wanted to focus on the client projects themselves. I had the vision that CYC would offer help (hard metrics) and not just advice. At the end of my first year, I saw that both our talent had been steadily declining and that our projects were able to offer minimal assistance given our knowledge and time constraints aka, I failed. Throughout summer 2015, an executive team and I underwent a CYC re-vamp. We envisioned a CYC that used design thinking, implementation, and iteration to offer maximum insight. This would allow us to move away from the typical management consulting space (of which Berkeley had a plethora of) and more towards a niche design thinking, cross-functional space that would differentiate ourselves from our peers. Secondly, we asked our advisors from major consulting and design firms what skills awesome intern candidates have and then began to develop a comprehensive curriculum on those skills for our new intern program. Sound interesting? Send me an email, let's connect!
